# WISHLIST


## New Features
- Some sort of way to indicate "yes, this planet is currently in the light sphere!" or "no, this planet is not currently in the light sphere!" Perhaps more generally, a panel or view where when you click on a celestial body, it gives you info. Like that body's share of the hash rate, its distance from the centroid, and whether it's in the sphere or not.
- Multiple levels of light-lag-sphere? How much is one impacted by its distance from the center? Ultimately, the sphere itself is... well, not arbitrary, but it doesn't tell the full picture. If a miner is within the sphere but at the very edge, they are still going to have a very tough time mining efficiently compared to someone much closer to the centroid, like anyone on the surface of the Earth. I could see additional light-lag-spheres being nested within or even on the outside, with the consequences of what they mean being illustrated in some way.
  - Being 50% of the way to the dge of the light sphere means 50% of the time the blocks you mine are outdated.
  - Being 200% of the way beyond the edge of the light sphere means you are physically restricted to being behind by two blocks.
  - These kinds of facts seem useful for this sim to outline.
- Restructure to support the two different "modes".
  - Mode 1 – Historical earth-based analysis. This would analyze the way the centroid has moved over time. This is why wwe have CCAF data, it's granular to the country level and illustrates cleanly how the China ban shifted the centroid around the center of the earth. This mode would be 100% hash rate on earth and would let you move the time slider to show how it has moved in reality over time, according to CCAF.
  - Mode 2 – Hypothetical interplanetary analysis. This would analyze what happens as the share of hashrate moves beyond earth to other bodies like the moon or Mars. You see, in this mode, the timeline and actual country-level granularity don't matter. This is why it feels like it needs a partition of the two different modes.
- Slice open a planet to reveal its core to more visually see where the centroid lies? this would be cool but just a bell/whistle, not really that useful for illustrating the point, I guess.
- Give the light-lag-sphere a mild pulse, as if to show how a light beam would travel through the sphere. Could even make it realistically timed since it's a 10-minute light sphere, that's not so slow that it would be undetectable, but also is slow enough that it could be very pleasant to see.
  - OH MAN, so what if instead we do this: I have been considering that this light sphere is an average. And that average is... not representative of reality. Its much like if someone said, "I have three shoes and two people, how many shoes does each person get?" The answer is 1.5. But what is .5 of a shoe? I mean you can literally cut it in half, but the point is that it doesn't map to anything useful in reality, because half of a shoe is useless. The same kind of concept applies here. Consider that there's 50% hash rate on earth and 50% hash rate on mars, and that earth and mars are 30 light minutes apart. The centorid would be 15 light minutes away from each, sitting in the middle. The light-lag-sphere would stretch out from this centroid in a spherical shape of radius 10 light minutes, leaving 5 light minutes between the edge of it and either planet on either side. Meaning the "average" exists in the middle, not on any planet. But think about the actual blocks being emitted in such a scenario. They both emit FROM a planet. The average is not reality, no single block ever gets emitted 15 light minutes awway from the nearest planet, they just can be averaged to that point.
  - So how do we reconcile this? What if we set up a second mode, where one is a mathematical purist/visualization mode that just renders the average, which is what we have today. And the other is a monte-carlo style simulation of blocks being emitted on each body according to their hashrate, USING that pulse-type behavior described above? So basically each block emitted would start as a tiny sphere on the planet that emits it and grows outwards to 10 light minutes before fading away. Its not that it fades away because that's when it stops (it doesn't, it goes on forever), it fades away because thats when _the next_ blcok would be emitted. Is this illustrative? I think so, but it would require some careful tuning to get right.
